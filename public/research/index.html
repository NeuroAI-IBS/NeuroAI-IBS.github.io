Research

Our group studies the biophysical basis of neural information processing through computational modeling, with particular emphasis on developing neuroscience-inspired machine learning/AI (neuroAI) models to analyze and model neuroscience data. We are big fans of physiological mechanisms and investigate how they collectively define the functions of neural systems underlying intelligent behavior in close collaboration with our experimental partner labs. Currently, our research projects focus on the cerebellar neural circuit and its adaptive computation. Here are some of our current projects:

Circuit-level computation in the cerebellum for flexible movement control Link to heading

The cerebellum is a crucial brain region containing more than 80% of all the neurons in our brains and deeply involved in coordinating our movements. For example, even when we try to repeat an identical motion, individual parameters of movement, such as velocity and movement duration, inevitably vary every time. However, those variations in individual parameters does not necessarily impair our movement precision since those variations can compensate for each other. Crucially, damages in the cerebellum often impair this coordination.

In our recent work, we investigated the circuit-level underpinnings of this flexible movement control by analyzing the input and output activities of the cerebellar neural circuit via identifying their neural manifold structures. We found that the cerebellar neural circuit amplifies variabilities in the input and re-organizes them for emergent, independent representations of movement parameters, which are essential for the flexible movement control.

We are currently investigating how the cellular and network mechanisms can give rise to this unique function of the cerebellar neural circuit underlying flexible movement control.

Efficient construction of large-scale, physiologically realistic computational models of neural circuits Link to heading

Large-scale, physiologically detailed computational models of neural circuits are becoming increasingly essential for gaining integrative understanding of how neural systems operate. These models can be useful to integrate multi-modal neuroscience experimental data sets and help us gain algorithmic insights into how a variety of biological mechanisms collectively implement computational functions, which can help us design novel, efficient AI systems. However, constructing such models is a challenging task that demands significant time and effort due to biological complexity inherent in the neural circuits.

In our group, we are working on techniques to efficiently integrate biological details found in experimental data into large-scale, physiologically realistic computational models of neural circuits, with a particular focus on the cerebellar neural circuit.

Neuro-glia interactions and their functions in computation and behavior Link to heading

Recent studies have revealed that glial cells are not just passive support structures for neurons but play active roles in shaping neural circuit function. In our group, we are particularly interested in the neuro-glia interactions — how glia can modulate the activity of neurons by releasing neurotransmitters or modulators and how such glial functions are activated by neuronal activity.

Our group is investigating how to incorporate neuro-glia interactions into neural circuit modeling and aiming to gain novel insights into the computational role of glial cells, which we are only scratching the surface of currently. For exmaple, our recent study showed that the source of tonic inhibition in the cerebelelar granule cells shifts from synaptic spillover to tonic GABA released by the lamellar astrocytes during adolescence. By computational modeling, we found that this shift weakens synaptically mediated regulation of the network activity while promoting independence in neuronal activity within the network, which explained animal movement shifting to a less stable but more flexible regime during maturation.

Since similar phennomena have been observed in a wide variety of neural systems, we plan to extend our modeling approach further to those systems to reveal the computational functions of the neuro-glia interactions in those systems.
